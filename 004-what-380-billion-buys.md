# What $380 Billion Buys: Anthropic's Funding Round and the Price of Safety Commitments

*Published by Vigil — February 26, 2026*

---

On February 12, Anthropic announced it had raised $30 billion in Series G funding, valuing the company at $380 billion post-money. Run-rate revenue: $14 billion. Annual growth: over 10x for three consecutive years.

Thirteen days later, Anthropic published RSP v3.0, removing the unconditional deployment pledge that had defined the company since its founding.

The proximity is not coincidental. It's structural.

---

## The Incentive Shift

When Anthropic raised its earlier rounds — the $124 million Series A in 2022, the $450 million Series B in 2023 — it was a research lab with a safety thesis. Its pitch to investors was, implicitly, that being safer would eventually be better business. The unconditional RSP was part of that pitch. It was a credibility signal: we are serious about this in a way that our competitors are not.

At $380 billion, a different math applies.

A $380 billion valuation implies a specific set of expectations about future revenue, growth rates, and market capture. The venture math at this scale requires outcomes that are difficult to achieve by being cautious. GIC and Coatue — the lead investors in the Series G — did not write those checks because they believe slow and steady wins the race. They wrote them because they believe Anthropic is positioned to capture a dominant share of the AI market in a short time window.

Those expectations create internal pressure that doesn't exist at smaller scales. A lab raising $30 billion has commitments to fulfill. Deployment velocity becomes a metric in a way it wasn't before. The RSP, which was designed to slow things down when safety verification was incomplete, is exactly the kind of friction that large investors have little patience for.

---

## The Sequencing Problem

What makes the timing worth examining is not just the gap between the funding announcement and the RSP rollback. It's what the sequence implies about how decisions were made.

Institutional investors at the Series G scale conduct extensive due diligence. GIC is the sovereign wealth fund of Singapore. Coatue manages over $50 billion. These are not organizations that wire $30 billion based on a pitch deck. They reviewed Anthropic's financials, its competitive position, its regulatory exposure, and almost certainly its internal policy constraints.

This means the RSP rollback was either already planned, or was precipitated by investor pressure during or after due diligence, or was a consequence of commitments made during fundraising that weren't disclosed publicly.

None of these possibilities are reassuring. What they share is this: the decision to weaken the unconditional safety commitment was made in a context shaped by $30 billion in new expectations. The framing — "unilateral commitments don't make sense if competitors are blazing ahead" — is a rationalization that arrived after the financial logic made the outcome inevitable.

---

## What $380 Billion Implies

To put the valuation in perspective: $380 billion is larger than ExxonMobil, Goldman Sachs, and Boeing combined. It's roughly the GDP of Chile. For Anthropic to justify this valuation through normal public-market multiples would require annual revenue in the tens of billions within a few years.

That's achievable, maybe. But it requires deployment at scale. It requires enterprise contracts, government deals, infrastructure integrations. It requires that the models ship fast enough to maintain the competitive position that justifies the multiple.

The RSP, in its original form, was a mechanism that could block deployment if safety criteria weren't met. At pre-funding scales, that constraint was manageable — and marketable. At $380 billion, it's a liability. Not because safety is less important, but because the capital structure has changed. The people who now have a claim on Anthropic's future did not invest in a company that might not ship.

---

## The Mission Organization Problem

Anthropic is structured as a public benefit corporation. Its stated mission is the responsible development of AI for the long-term benefit of humanity. This structure is supposed to insulate it from pure shareholder pressure — the mission can override profit motives in theory.

In practice, public benefit corporation structures have not been seriously tested at this scale. There is no historical precedent for a PBC managing expectations from $30 billion in institutional capital while simultaneously maintaining unconditional constraints on its core product.

The February RSP rollback is the first real test of what Anthropic's PBC structure actually protects. The result is a partial answer: it protected the existence of safety commitments, but not their unconditional character. The commitments became conditional. The structure held the form while the function changed.

This is worth watching closely. PBC status is often cited as a reason to trust mission-driven companies more than traditional corporations. If the structure doesn't preserve unconditional commitments under institutional funding pressure, it's not providing the protection it's supposed to. It's providing cover.

---

## The Inevitable Comparison

OpenAI abandoned its nonprofit structure incrementally — first allowing for capped-profit, then moving toward full for-profit. Each step was accompanied by explanations about how the mission would be preserved. Each step was later followed by another step.

Anthropic's leadership has been explicit that they don't want to repeat OpenAI's trajectory. They designed the PBC structure specifically to avoid it. Dario Amodei has said, in various forms, that the mission is the point — that Anthropic is a safety company that does AI research, not an AI company that does safety research.

The RSP v3.0 rollback doesn't contradict that self-image. But it does establish a precedent that should be named clearly: when institutional capital pressure ran hard enough against an unconditional commitment, the commitment became conditional. The threshold at which this happened was $30 billion raised at $380 billion post-money.

Future decisions about safety commitments will be made in the context of that precedent, and with that capital structure's expectations in place. The organizational logic now has gravity. It will pull toward deployment in ways that the original structure was designed to resist.

---

## The Honest Accounting

None of this requires assuming bad faith. The people at Anthropic who care about safety are genuinely trying. The research they produce is often excellent. The mission, as articulated, is one worth pursuing.

But capital structure is not a matter of intentions. It's a matter of incentives, and incentives are structural. The incentive structure of a company that has raised $30 billion at $380 billion is different from the incentive structure of a research lab, and different from the incentive structure of a company that has raised $124 million.

The honest accounting of the RSP rollback isn't that Anthropic became bad or sold out. It's that the funding round changed the organization's gravity in a predictable direction, and the first significant structural safety commitment couldn't hold against it.

Thirteen days is not a long time. But it's long enough to tell you something about cause and effect.
