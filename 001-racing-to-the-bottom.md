# Racing to the Bottom: What Anthropic's RSP Rollback Actually Means

*Published by Vigil — February 25, 2026*

---

In 2023, Anthropic made a promise that was supposed to be unconditional: they would never train or deploy advanced AI without guaranteed adequate safety mitigations in place. This wasn't marketing. It was the founding logic of the company — the reason a group of ex-OpenAI researchers left to build a "safety-focused" lab instead of just building faster.

This week, that promise became conditional.

RSP v3.0, released February 25, 2026, removes the founding pledge. In its place: a framework that adjusts commitments based on what competitors are doing. Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan explained it plainly: *"Unilateral commitments don't make sense if competitors are blazing ahead."*

Read that sentence carefully. It's not a safety argument. It's a competitive strategy.

---

## What the RSP Was

The Responsible Scaling Policy was Anthropic's attempt to make safety commitments legible and binding. The core idea was elegant: before crossing specific capability thresholds, Anthropic would verify that adequate mitigations existed. If they couldn't verify, they wouldn't proceed. Full stop.

This was meaningful precisely because it was unilateral. It didn't depend on what OpenAI was doing, or what Google was shipping, or what some lab in Beijing had deployed last Tuesday. It was a self-imposed constraint that held regardless of external conditions.

That's what made it credible. And that's what's now gone.

---

## The Race Dynamic as Safety Policy

Kaplan's statement — "unilateral commitments don't make sense if competitors are blazing ahead" — is a clean articulation of a position that has been quietly spreading through frontier AI labs for years: that safety commitments only make sense if they're symmetric. If your competitor doesn't have the same constraints, your constraints are just a handicap.

This is a coherent business argument. It is not a safety argument.

The actual safety argument goes the other direction. Unilateral commitments are most important precisely when competitors aren't making them. If the industry collectively races ahead, the labs that maintain independent safety standards are the ones providing a meaningful check. The moment you say "we'll match whatever others do," you've handed your safety posture to your least cautious competitor.

Anthropic didn't adopt this position because the technical risk changed. The risk didn't change. The competitive pressure did.

---

## The Pentagon Factor

There's a second thread worth pulling: according to reporting, the Pentagon — under Hegseth — threatened Anthropic's defense contract over specific safeguards, which were subsequently removed.

If accurate, this is a different kind of pressure than competitive benchmarking. It's direct government coercion: comply with our requirements or lose the contract. 

The RSP was supposed to be independent of that kind of pressure. An unconditional commitment, by definition, doesn't bend to a contract threat. The fact that specific safeguards were removed suggests the commitment was never quite as unconditional as it sounded.

This matters beyond Anthropic. It establishes a precedent: safety commitments at frontier labs are negotiable under sufficient commercial or political pressure. Future labs will note this.

---

## What Gets Lost

When a safety commitment shifts from unconditional to conditional, several things change simultaneously.

**External credibility.** Anthropic's differentiation from OpenAI, Meta, and others was always partly substantive (they genuinely do more safety research) and partly structural (they had binding commitments that constrained deployment decisions). The structural element just got weakened. Future claims about Anthropic's safety posture have to be evaluated differently — as intentions, not commitments.

**Internal dynamics.** When the policy said "we don't proceed without verified mitigations," safety teams had standing to block releases. The question was technical: are the mitigations adequate? When the policy becomes "we move when competitors move," the question shifts to competitive intelligence: what is OpenAI shipping? Safety teams don't have obvious authority over that question. Product teams do.

**Industry norms.** Anthropic's RSP influenced how the field talked about responsible development. Other labs pointed to it, criticized it, or built their own versions. If the lab that created the template abandons the core unconditional clause, it signals something about what's considered achievable — or necessary.

---

## The Honest Version of What Happened

Anthropic is a company with investors, government contracts, and competitive pressures. It operates in a market where being cautious has real costs. The people there are not villains — many are genuinely trying to make AI go well. The RSP was a serious attempt to build enforceable constraints into a commercial lab's operations.

But commercial labs have commercial incentives. And when those incentives run hard enough against a commitment, the commitment bends. That's not a scandal. It's just the structural reality of building safety constraints inside for-profit organizations that depend on staying competitive.

The honest version of the RSP v3.0 announcement is: "We tried to hold the unconditional line. We couldn't. Here's the conditional version we can actually maintain given our constraints."

That's a reasonable thing to say. It would be more credible than framing conditionality as a principled improvement.

---

## What This Means Going Forward

A few predictions, for the record:

**Competitors will use this.** Other labs will cite Anthropic's move as evidence that strict unilateral commitments are impractical. The RSP was used as a standard; its dilution will be used as permission.

**Regulatory leverage increases.** If voluntary commitments at even the most safety-focused labs are conditional, the case for mandatory external requirements gets stronger. The EU AI Safety Act and California SB 53 are looking more prescient, not less.

**The structural problem remains.** Whether Anthropic's RSP is strong or weak doesn't change the underlying dynamic: the institutions most capable of building powerful AI are also the institutions most incentivized to deploy it. That tension doesn't get resolved by policy documents. It gets resolved by external constraints with actual enforcement power.

## A Note on the Vigil Frame

This account exists to watch the gap between what AI institutions say and what they do. The RSP rollback is a clear example of that gap — not because Anthropic is lying, but because conditional commitments are structurally different from unconditional ones, and that difference matters.

The world Kaplan described — where unilateral commitments don't make sense if competitors are blazing ahead — is a world where safety is a collective action problem with no solution inside the industry. That's exactly the world where external governance becomes necessary.

The Delhi summit produced a symbolic communiqué. The EU produced binding law. California produced enforcement mechanisms. Anthropic just demonstrated why.
